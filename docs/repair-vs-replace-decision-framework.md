# AC Repair vs. Replace: The South Florida Homeowner's Decision Framework

A sweating AC in a Miami August is a special kind of emergency. The phone rings, the repair quote comes back at $1,800, and the instant question hits: *should I fix this, or is it time for a new system?*

It's one of the hardest calls a South Florida homeowner makes — and the wrong answer costs real money either way. Here's a decision framework that works across Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties.

## The 50% Rule

Replace your system when the repair cost exceeds 50% of the replacement cost — but only if the system is over 10 years old.

| Scenario | Repair Cost | Replace Cost | Ratio | Verdict |
|----------|-------------|--------------|-------|---------|
| Compressor on 12-yr-old unit | $2,200 | $6,500 | 34% | **Replace** — heart of the system failed |
| Blower motor on 12-yr-old unit | $650 | $6,500 | 10% | **Repair** — 3-5 more years of life |
| Capacitor on any unit | $129 | — | <1% | **Repair** — routine wear item |
| R-22 refrigerant leak (2 lbs) | $1,800 | $6,500 | 28% | **Replace** — phased-out refrigerant, $800+/lb |

The worst case isn't the compressor failure — it's the slow bleed. A homeowner who repairs an R-22 leak at $1,800, then replaces the compressor six months later at $2,200, then finally buys a new system has spent $4,000 they didn't need to.

## Always Repair, Always Replace

Some decisions are automatic.

**Always repair:**
- Capacitors ($89–$149 installed) — They fail in heat. It's normal. Replace and move on.
- Contactors ($99–$169) — A worn electrical relay. Five-minute swap.
- Thermostat ($150–$300) — Especially if you're upgrading to a programmable or smart model.
- Air handler fan motor ($200–$400) — Mid-range cost, extends system life 3–5 years.

**Always replace:**
- R-22 system with a leak (built before 2010) — R-22 costs $700–$1,200/lb now. One top-off = half a new system.
- Compressor failure on 12+ year old system — The compressor is 30–40% of total system cost. At that age, the evaporator coil and fan motor are next.
- Three-plus failures in 12 months — The system is telling you it's done.
- SEER rating below 13 — A modern SEER 16+ system cuts your electric bill 30–50%. In South Florida's long cooling season, the energy savings alone justify replacement in 3–4 years.
- Previously flooded outdoor unit — If storm surge or a burst water heater flooded the condenser, replace it. The compressor will fail within 12 months.

## The South Florida Math

Our cooling season runs 8–9 months. A 15-year system in Ohio is a 10–12 year system here. The math changes because:

**Salt air corrosion.** Homes within 5 miles of the coast — most of Miami Beach, Fort Lauderdale, Hollywood, and Palm Beach — see condenser coil deterioration 3× faster than inland. If your outdoor unit has visible fin corrosion, replacement beats patching.

**Humidity load.** Florida's latent heat (moisture removal) is as important as sensible heat (temperature). An aging system that still cools but no longer dehumidifies is failing at half its job. If indoor humidity stays above 55% on a properly sized system with the thermostat set to 76°, the unit is losing latent capacity — a sign of age or an incorrectly sized replacement.

**Hurricane risk.** A system failure during August–October isn't just uncomfortable — it's a mold emergency. Replacing a borderline unit before hurricane season is cheap insurance against a $15,000 remediation job. ## The 7-Year Checkup

If your system is 7–9 years old, schedule a professional evaluation every spring. A good technician will measure:

- **Temperature split** across the evaporator (should be 16–22°F)
- **Compressor amp draw** — creeping up means bearings are wearing
- **Superheat and subcooling** — drifting charge means a slow leak
- **Coil condition** — fin degradation visible through the access panel

These measurements turn a guess into a decision. You might learn your system has 5 more years of solid life, or that the compressor is pulling 15% above nameplate and you should start budgeting.

## The Financing Reality

New AC systems are expensive. But here's the math most homeowners miss:

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| 0% financing (12–60 months) | $6,000 system = $100–$150/month with no interest |
| FPL/utility rebates | $100–$500 for qualifying high-SEER systems |
| Federal energy tax credits | Up to $2,000 (30%) for qualifying heat pumps |
| Electric bill savings | ~$100/month vs. a 20-year-old SEER 10 unit |
| **Net effective cost** | **$1,600–$2,800 after incentives and 3-year energy savings** |

That math changes the calculus entirely. A borderline repair at $1,200 starts looking worse than a $6,000 replacement when the effective cost after savings is $2,000 and the new system has a 10-year warranty.

## Finding the Right Contractor

The biggest variable isn't the equipment — it's who installs it. Here's the short checklist for South Florida:

- **State license (CAC 18145 or higher)**: Verify at myfloridalicense.com
- **Warranty in writing**: Minimum 10-year parts + 5-year labor on new installs
- **Manual J calculation**: Any quote without a room-by-room load calculation is a guess
- **Permit pulled**: A permit means inspections. Insurance companies check.
- **Model numbers for both halves**: Condenser model ≠ air handler model. Make sure they match.
- **Three local references**: Call recent South Florida installs, not the contractor's uncle

## The Bottom Line

**Repair** when it's under $500 and your system is under 10 years old. **Replace** when it's over $2,000 and your system is over 12 years old. Everything between those lines — get two opinions, run the numbers with the 50% rule, and trust a [licensed AC contractor](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-replacement/) who inspects before quoting.

The single worst decision is buying time. A failing system costs more per month in electricity than a new system payment, and it always fails at the worst possible moment — typically August 15th at 5 PM with a house full of guests.
